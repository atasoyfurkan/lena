What Is DIabetes?

Diabetes is a disease that occurs when your blood glucose, also called blood sugar, is too high. Diabetes Mellitus (DM), known as diabetes, is a lifelong chronic disease that results from the inability of the pancreas to produce enough insulin or the insulin production of the body to reduce, and the cells that produce insulin decrease. As a result, people with Diabetes cannot use the glucose that passes blood from the foods they eat and blood glucose (blood sugar) level rise (hiperglisemi).

Most of the food we eat, especially those containing carbohydrates, are converted into glucose to produce energy that body needs. The pancreas, an organ located on the back of the stomach, releases a hormone called "insulin" that allows our muscles and other tissues to take glucose from the blood and use it as energy. When the nutrients break down in the intestine, glucose enters the cells through the hormone called insulin. Cells use glucose as a source of energy. If the amount of glucose in the blood is more than the body's need for energy, it is stored in fat tissues in the liver.

The blood sugar level of an individual without diabetes does not exceed 120 mg/dl when feeling hunger and 140 mg/dl in fullness (two hours after starting to eat). A blood glucose level measured above these values indicates the presence of diabetes.

A person's diabetes is determined by measuring preprandial blood glucose or by Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT). Having a preprandial blood glucose measurement of 100-125 mg/dl is a secret sugar (pre-diabetes) signal. A preprandial blood glucose measurement result of 126 mg/dl or more indicates the presence of diabetes.

For OGTT, blood glucose value after taking glucose-rich fluid in two hours is important. If the blood glucose measurement in the second hour is 140-199 mg/dl, we can talk about hidden diabetes. If it is 200 mg/dl or higher, diabetes is diagnosed.
