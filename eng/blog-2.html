How to measure your blood sugar/glucose at home?

What is Self-Control? Why is It Important?

In order to ensure metabolic control in diabetes, the patient's self-control of the value of ketone, glycemia, and glycosuria is defined as self-monitoring or home monitoring. It provides easy and inexpensive glycemic control for diabetic patients. Detection of hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia attacks and taking necessary precautions are also important for the early diagnosis of complications and delaying or preventing their development. It helps patients organize their life in connection with diet, exercise, and blood sugar levels reduce the frequency and duration of hospitalization. It provides a more flexible life. Self-monitoring greatly reduces the cost of diabetes in the short and long term.

When Should You Test Your Blood Sugar?

The needs of the patient determine the frequency of blood glucose measurement. Diabetics who use insulin pumps or insulin injections and who are pregnant should measure glycemia 4 times daily until glycemic control is achieved. It is recommended to take measurements before breakfast, lunch, dinner, and night meal. Once glycemic control is achieved, the frequency of measurements can be reduced to 3-4 per week. Type-2 diabetics who do not have glycemic control and do not use insulin should control postprandial glycemia before breakfast and 2 hours after a meal. Type-2 diabetics with glycemic control can only measure glucose in urine to control glycemia.

What is a Diabetes Diary? How to Use It?

The diabetes diary is a notebook used by people with diabetes to record their process of diabetes and self-monitoring. These records should include the date, time, result as well as the time and dose of insulin or other orally taken pills. Also, any event that is thought to affect the outcome of glycemia (change in a meal and/or physical activity level, stress) should be included. Taking regular diabetes diary and recording this information accurately is useful and important to re-plan the treatment process and to be evaluated by a physician, dietitian, and diabetes educator. You need to show your diabetes diary to your doctor during your treatment.
